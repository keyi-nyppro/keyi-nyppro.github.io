üìú Origin

Xiangqi, is also known as Chinese Chess, is a strategy board game that traces its roots back over 1,000 years, making it one of the oldest forms of chess in the world.

  • The word "Ë±°Ê£ã" literally means "Elephant Game" or "Image Game".
  • It is believed to have evolved from Chaturanga, an ancient Indian board game that also gave rise to Western Chess and Shogi (Japanese Chess).
  • Xiangqi developed independently in China, incorporating local military culture, philosophy, and symbolism.

Historical Timeline üìÜ

Warring States (475 - 221 BCE)

Early strategic ear simulations were played by generals. Some scholars trace Xiangqi's concept to this era.

Han Dynasty (206 BCE - 220 CE)

First references to a photo-chess game appear in literature.

Tang Dynasty (618 - 907)

Xiangqi begins to take a form closer to what we know today. The game's popularity spreads among scholats and nobles.

Song Dynasty (960 - 1279)

Xiangqi becomes standardized with a layout including the river, palace, and distinct pieces. Manuals and strategy books begin to appear.

Ming & Qing Dynasties (1368 - 1912)

The game is fully codified. It becomes a favorite leisure activity across all social classes.

Mordern Era

Xiangqi is widely played across China, Taiwan, Vietnam, and Chinese communities worldwide. International tournaments and online platforms bring global recognition.
